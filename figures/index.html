Key Figures & Sects

Gnosticism was shaped by influential teachers, mystics, and organized movements. This page profiles the most important Gnostic figures and schools of thought that emerged across the Mediterranean world from the 2nd to 4th centuries.

Valentinus

c. 100-160 CE

Role: Major Gnostic theologian and teacher

Valentinus was one of the most influential Gnostic thinkers of the 2nd century. Born in Egypt, he later moved to Rome where he taught and developed a complex cosmological system.

Key Teachings:

  • Developed an elaborate system of Aeons (divine emanations)
  • Described the Pleroma (fullness of the divine realm) and its structure
  • Taught that the material world was created by a lesser deity, not the true God
  • Emphasized gnosis as salvation through knowledge of one's divine spark

Legacy: Valentinianism became the most organized and widespread Gnostic movement, surviving well into the 4th century with numerous schools and interpretations.

Basilides

c. 117-138 CE

Role: Early Gnostic philosopher and systematizer

One of the earliest major Gnostic teachers, Basilides taught in Alexandria and developed one of the first comprehensive Gnostic systems.

Key Teachings:

  • Proposed a hierarchy of 365 heavens
  • Distinguished between the supreme God and the demiurge (creator god)
  • Taught that suffering and ignorance resulted from past karmic actions
  • Developed theories of predestination and spiritual election

Legacy: Basilidianism influenced later Gnostic thought and was widely debated by Church Fathers. His system influenced concepts of divine hierarchy that appeared in various mystical traditions.

Marcion

c. 85-160 CE

Role: Christian theologian and radical reformer

Though sometimes classified separately from Gnosticism, Marcion shared many Gnostic concepts and created one of the earliest alternative Christian canons.

Key Teachings:

  • Argued the God of the Old Testament was different from the God of Jesus
  • Created his own New Testament canon (excluding the Old Testament entirely)
  • Rejected the material creation as evil or ignorant
  • Emphasized radical dualism between spirit and matter

Legacy: Marcionism represented a major challenge to emerging orthodoxy. His ideas influenced how the Church defined the canon and addressed dualistic theology.

Sophia (The Wisdom Figure)

Mythological figure

Role: Divine feminine principle in Gnostic cosmology

Sophia represents divine wisdom and is a central mythological figure in many Gnostic systems, particularly in the cosmogonic myths that explain creation.

Key Teachings:

  • Often portrayed as an Aeon who fell or descended
  • Her desire or passion created the material world
  • Trapped within matter, imprisoned in human souls as divine spark
  • Represents both divine wisdom and the consequence of transgression

Legacy: The figure of Sophia became central to Gnostic anthropology and cosmology. She appears in numerous Gnostic texts as a crucial mediator between the divine and material realms.

Major Gnostic Movements

Valentinianism

2nd-4th centuries

The most organized and influential Gnostic school. Valentinian thought spread throughout the Mediterranean and developed numerous sub-schools with varying interpretations.

Characteristics: Elaborate Aeon systems, sophisticated cosmology, emphasis on inner gnosis, organized community structure.

Key Texts: Gospel of Philip, Gospel of Truth, Apocryphon of John (disputed)

Basilidianism

2nd-4th centuries

An early systematized Gnostic movement originating in Alexandria. Known for its complex metaphysical hierarchies and unique interpretations of suffering.

Characteristics: Hierarchical cosmology, karmic theory, special gnosis reserved for the elect, numerical mysticism.

Key Texts: Lost texts known primarily through Church Father accounts

Sethianism

2nd-4th centuries

Named for its reverence of Seth (the third son of Adam), this school incorporated Jewish mysticism into Gnostic frameworks and influenced Jewish mystical traditions.

Characteristics: Emphasis on Seth as divine figure, incorporation of Jewish texts, complex emanation systems, visionary experiences.

Key Texts: Apocryphon of John, Hypostasis of the Archons, Three Steles of Seth

Nassenes (Serpent Worshippers)

2nd-3rd centuries

A group that venerated the serpent as a symbol of divine wisdom, drawing on pre-Christian mystery traditions and reinterpreting biblical narratives.

Characteristics: Mystical practices, reinterpretation of serpent symbolism, synthesis of pagan and Christian elements, ecstatic experiences.

Key Texts: Known mainly through Hippolytus's polemical account

Ophites (Snake Sect)

2nd-4th centuries

Various groups that valorized the serpent of Genesis as a liberating force of knowledge against the tyrannical demiurge.

Characteristics: Revaluation of biblical narrative, veneration of wisdom symbols, dualistic cosmology, antinomian ethics in some groups.

Key Texts: Apocryphon of John, Testimony of Truth

Marcionism

2nd-5th centuries

A radical Christian movement that saw a fundamental opposition between the God of the Old Testament and the Father of Jesus, creating alternative scriptural canon.

Characteristics: Dualistic theology, alternative canon, organized church structure, radical rejection of Jewish scriptures, celibacy and asceticism.

Key Texts: Marcion's Gospel (reduced Luke), selected Pauline Epistles

Relationship to Orthodox Christianity

Gnostic figures and sects represented significant challenges to emerging Christian orthodoxy in several ways:

  • Cosmology: Gnostics rejected the idea that the creator God was the supreme good God, threatening monotheistic theology
  • Scripture: Many Gnostic teachers created alternative canons or reinterpreted canonical texts, challenging scriptural authority
  • Salvation: Gnostic emphasis on esoteric knowledge conflicted with orthodox emphasis on faith and the Church's teaching authority
  • Matter and Spirit: Gnostic dualism contrasted sharply with orthodox affirmations of creation's goodness and bodily resurrection
  • Authority: Gnostic emphasis on personal revelation and secret teachings challenged hierarchical Church structures
