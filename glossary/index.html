Glossary of Terms

A comprehensive reference guide to specialized Gnostic terminology, theological concepts, historical terms, and ancient language translations.

Aeon

A divine or celestial being emanating from the Godhead; also refers to an age or eon in the cosmological hierarchy of Gnostic systems.

Etymology: Greek: αἰών (aión), meaning "age" or "eternity"

Archon

A ruler or chief of a lower cosmic realm; in Gnostic thought, archons are often malevolent beings who rule the material world and prevent human spiritual ascension.

Etymology: Greek: ἄρχων (árkhōn), meaning "ruler" or "chief"

Demiurge

The creator deity of the material world in Gnostic cosmology; often viewed as inferior to the true God and sometimes portrayed as ignorant or malevolent. Associated with the Old Testament God YHWH in some systems.

Etymology: Greek: δημιουργός (dēmiourgós), meaning "craftsman" or "maker"

Gnosis

Direct, experiential knowledge of the divine; the salvific knowledge that leads to liberation from the material world and union with the transcendent God. Central to Gnostic spirituality.

Etymology: Greek: γνῶσις (gnôsis), meaning "knowledge" or "understanding"

Monad

The supreme, transcendent source of all being; the ultimate, unknowable God in Gnostic cosmology. Often conceived as existing beyond all categories and attributes.

Etymology: Greek: μονάς (monás), meaning "unity" or "single unit"

Pleroma

The fullness of the divine realm; the totality of aeons and divine emanations in Gnostic cosmology. Contrasts with the material world or "kenoma" (void).

Etymology: Greek: πλήρωμα (plḗrōma), meaning "fullness" or "completeness"

Sophia

Wisdom personified as a divine feminine principle; in Gnostic myth, Sophia's act of passionate desire causes the emanation of the demiurge and the fall into matter.

Etymology: Greek: σοφία (sophía), meaning "wisdom"

Hylic

Relating to matter or the material; in Gnostic anthropology, hylics are humans composed entirely of matter who lack the divine spark and cannot achieve gnosis.

Etymology: Greek: ὕλη (hýlē), meaning "matter" or "wood"

Psychic

Relating to the soul; in Gnostic anthropology, psychics are humans with a soul who possess the capacity for faith and can potentially achieve salvation through education and gnosis.

Etymology: Greek: ψυχή (psychḗ), meaning "soul"

Pneumatic

Relating to spirit; in Gnostic anthropology, pneumatics are humans endowed with the divine spark and capable of achieving gnosis and salvation.

Etymology: Greek: πνεῦμα (pneûma), meaning "spirit" or "breath"

Nag Hammadi Library

A collection of thirteen ancient codices discovered in 1945 near the town of Nag Hammadi in Upper Egypt, containing fifty-two texts primarily from Gnostic, Hermetic, and early Christian sources from the 4th century CE.

Cathar

Members of a dualistic heretical sect in medieval Europe (12th-14th centuries) who held beliefs similar to Gnosticism, including dualism and the rejection of material creation as evil.

Council of Nicaea

An ecumenical council of Christian bishops convened in 325 CE by Roman Emperor Constantine to establish orthodoxy and counter heresies including Gnosticism and Arianism.

Coptic

The last stage of the Egyptian language, written in the Coptic script (a modified Greek alphabet); the language of most texts in the Nag Hammadi Library.

Koine Greek

The common form of Greek that emerged after Alexander the Great's conquests; the dialect used in early Christian texts and many Gnostic writings.

Kenoma

The void or emptiness; in Gnostic cosmology, the material world or realm outside the pleroma, often conceived as inferior and defective.

Etymology: Greek: κένωμα (kénōma), meaning "emptiness" or "void"

Emanation

In Gnostic theology, the process by which the divine produces successive levels of being, from the transcendent Monad through progressively lower aeons and ultimately matter.

Cosmogony

An account or theory of the origin of the cosmos; Gnostic cosmogonies describe the creation of the material world as a flawed or fallen process.

This glossary is continuously updated with new terms and refined definitions. For more detailed exploration, consult the primary texts and scholarly resources available throughout this site.
